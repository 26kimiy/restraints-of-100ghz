Difficulty with 100 GHz

  1. Conductor loss grows as √f because the skin effect packs the current into a layer only about 0.2 µm thick at 100 GHz, so the metal looks steadily more resistive. By 100 GHz the loss runs from about 5.6 dB/m for a large coax up to roughly 200 dB/m for a chip-scale CPW (see the snapshot below).
  2. The three metals sit almost on top of each other. Going Cu → Ag → Au changes the loss by at most about 20% (silver a hair better than copper, gold about 20% worse). Silver is the best conductor that exists and it barely helps, so you cannot escape by picking a better metal; the metal itself is the wall.
  3. The line type spreads far more than the metal. A large coax stays near 5 dB/m while a chip-scale CPW is near 200 dB/m, about 35× worse, purely because its tiny conductors crowd the current into less metal. At 100 GHz you are pushed onto exactly those small on-chip lines, so you are stuck high on the graph.

Bottom line: on the small planar lines you are forced to use at 100 GHz, the loss is set by the metal's skin effect, and conductor loss far outweighs the dielectric loss. There is no better metal to switch to, so the metal interconnect, not the insulator, is the bottleneck.

The bridge from the Drude model

The fast current crowds into a thin surface skin, so the metal presents a surface resistance Rs, the real part of its surface impedance [1]. We feed it the full Drude conductivity σ(ω) = σdc/(1 − iωτ) from the parameters page [3], so the curves stay exact across the whole band:

Rs = Re √iωμ0σ(ω)

At low frequency σ(ω) → σdc and this reduces to the familiar Rs = √(π f μ0 / σdc) ∝ √f; at 100 GHz the two agree to about 1%, and the gap grows to roughly 8% by 1 THz (up to about 13% for silver). Conductor loss for each line is Rs times a geometry factor; dielectric loss comes from the substrate tanδ (not from the metal).

Symbol keyαc, αd: loss per meter from the conductor and from the dielectric (×8.686 turns Np/m into dB/m).
Rs: surface resistance, how lossy the metal surface looks to the fast current; bigger means more loss.
δ: skin depth, the thin surface layer the current crowds into.
σ: the metal's conductivity (how well it conducts).  ρ: resistivity = 1/σ.
f: frequency.  ω: angular frequency = 2πf.  k0: free-space wavenumber = 2πf/c.
μ0: permeability of free space (a fixed constant).  c: speed of light.
a, b (coax): inner-wire radius and outer-tube inner radius.
η: wave impedance of the insulator = η0/√εr.  η0 ≈ 377 Ω (free space).
εr: dielectric constant of the insulator (PTFE ≈ 2.1, alumina ≈ 9.9).  εeff: effective value when the field is split between substrate and air.
tanδ: loss tangent, the insulator's leakiness (small = good).
Z0: characteristic impedance (50 Ω here).  W: strip width.  ln(b/a): natural log of the size ratio.
CPW: S center-strip width, g gap, k = S/(S+2g), a = S/2, b = S/2+g, t metal thickness; K(k) a special function (elliptic integral) from the conformal mapping.
