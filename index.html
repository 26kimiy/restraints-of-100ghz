Restraints of 100 GHz

This brief examines the principal constraints on transmitting and measuring a signal at 100 GHz: their physical origin, their scaling with frequency, established mitigation techniques, a comparative analysis of copper against alternative transmission media, and a complete set of citations.

The most prominent inhibitors are:
  1. Skin effect (current crammed into a 0.2 µm surface skin)1
  2. Dielectric loss (directly proportional to frequency)4
  3. Source phase noise (multiplied up along with the signal)6

Well-accepted improvements

Differential signaling

Send the signal twice, normal and inverted, on a matched pair. Noise hits both equally, so the receiver subtracts it out and the wanted difference survives.

Works: DC to ~tens of GHz (USB, PCIe, HDMI, DDR). Stops: past ~50 GHz the pair drifts out of match and noise leaks back; and it does nothing for the wires' own loss.

Shielding & grounding

Wrap the conductor in a grounded shield: coax surrounds it; stripline sits between ground planes. The metal catches stray fields and blocks radiation.

Works: at every frequency for keeping noise out and signal in. Stops: it does nothing for the wire's own loss, and by 100 GHz it must shrink toward waveguide size.

Dielectric loss

αcopper = 4.34 · ( RDC + √ω · Rs ) / Z0ω = 2πf, grows ∝ √f
αdielectric = 4.34 · ω · C · Z0 · tanδω = 2πf out front, grows ∝ f

How dielectric loss works: picture the line as two conductors with an insulator stretched between them; the insulator absorbs a little energy from the electric field and turns it into heat. It is full of tiny molecular dipoles that twist to line up with the field. With an AC signal the field flips billions of times a second, so the dipoles must flip too, but they cannot flip instantly: they have inertia and bump against their neighbours, so they lag behind the field. That lag is the whole story, because the energy spent twisting them is not fully returned when the field reverses, like internal friction or a slightly sticky spring. The lost fraction shows up as heat, and the size of that lag is what tanδ measures.4

Improvement: low-tanδ dielectrics such as PTFE (Teflon) or fused silica, a modest cost premium over ordinary board. Works for: into the tens of GHz; past that only hollow waveguide or fiber escapes it.

some notes for me:

permittivity ε = ε′ − jε″: ε′ is the normal energy-storing response (in phase, lossless); the imaginary part ε″ is the lagging, energy-dissipating part (out of phase, lossy).

tanδ = ε″ / ε′
linear nature: (loss per cycle) × (cycles per second) ∝ f

Your hand

✋
reading shifts
a hand moves near the probe

Your hand is mostly water, which has a high permittivity and is lossy. Held near the line it acts as a dielectric load: it detunes the line, absorbs energy, and reflects some back, so the trace moves before you even touch it.426 Its warmth also slowly heats the setup (a small thermal-drift effect), but the instant jump is the dielectric loading.

Improvement: line the fixture with RF absorber, hands kept clear (cheap). Works for: fully, as long as nothing enters the field.
Source & instrument

Phase-noise multiplication

We do not have oscillators that run cleanly at 100 GHz. So you take one good low-frequency oscillator (say ~10 GHz) and run it through a frequency multiplier, a circuit that multiplies its frequency by N (here ×10) to reach 100 GHz. The catch: multiplying the frequency by N multiplies its timing jitter (phase noise) by N too, which is +20·log(N) dB, so a ×10 multiply adds +20 dB of noise.6

And no oscillator is perfectly steady to start with. Even our best, lab-only optical atomic clocks hold frequency to about 1 part in 1018;21 everyday microwave references are far noisier, and the multiplier only makes it worse.

Improvement: optical frequency combs build the tone with photonics instead of multiplying, avoiding the 20logN penalty, though they are very expensive and lab-grade. Works for: lab and high-end photonic systems; ordinary multiplier chains always pay the penalty.

some notes for me:

carrier output = cos(Nωt + Nφ(t))
frequency = Nω
phase deviation, noise = Nφ
signal / carrier power = (cos(Nωt + Nφ(t)))²
noise power = N² · ⟨φ²⟩
decibel change relative to carrier, dBc = 10·log₁₀(N²)
→ carrier power unchanged; phase-noise power relative to the carrier up by N² (or +20·log N)6
From the environment

Connector repeatability

Micron-scale misalignment when you re-mate a connector is a big fraction of a 3 mm wavelength, so the same measurement isn't the same twice.8

Improvement: precision connectors (1.0 mm and 0.8 mm) tightened with a torque wrench, which are pricey precision parts. Works for: 1.0 mm to ~110 GHz, 0.8 mm to ~145 GHz; above that, waveguide or on-wafer probing.
In the geometry

Radiation leakage & bends

Bending or folding the cable creates discontinuities and changes its electrical length. Even a good cable shifts about 0.2°×f(GHz) of phase per flex (~20° at 100 GHz), and moving it after calibration makes the cal stale.910

And at a 3 mm wavelength every such discontinuity, a fold, a bend, or a connector, acts like a tiny antenna and radiates signal into the room.8

Improvement: a coaxial cable seals the signal in a grounded shield so it cannot radiate (cheap and everywhere); use phase-stable cables and lock them down so bends do not move (mid-range cost). Works for: coax contains the signal up to ~110 GHz (then waveguide); locking the cable greatly cuts flex error, but any move after calibration still invalidates it.
From the environment

Thermal drift

Metal expands; a tiny temperature change shifts the electrical length enough to move the phase at a 3 mm wavelength.910

slide: cooler → hotter (copper microstructure, illustrative)
Rough estimate: at 100 GHz a 1 m cable holds about 470 wavelengths, so ~170,000° of total phase. With an electrical-length drift of roughly 20 ppm/°C, every 1°C swings the phase by about 3.4°. A hand resting on it, warming a stretch by only ~3°C, shifts the reading by about 10° of phase, enough to corrupt a measurement. (Thermal drift shows up as a phase shift, not an amplitude change.)910
Improvement: phase-stable cables and oven-controlled (OCXO) references hold the length and the clock steady, at a moderate off-the-shelf cost. Works for: lab conditions; cuts drift sharply but never to zero.
Source & instrument

Vanishing output power

The THz band (~0.1-10 THz) sits between electronics below (transistors, diodes) and photonics above (lasers). Both run out of steam approaching it, so output power sags deep in the middle: the power gap.12

Electronics hit their top usable frequency and bleed power to losses and multiplier conversion; photonics fail because a THz photon (~4 meV at 1 THz) is far below room-temperature thermal energy (~26 meV), so a laser can barely hold its inversion. Compact sources end up at µW to a few mW while uses want mW to W, and a weak signal against a fixed, insensitive detector noise floor means poor SNR.

Benchmarks: at 100 GHz, GaN amplifiers reach ~4-5 W and multiplier chains ~100-150 mW (mW then µW above ~300 GHz); the gear is costly, one W-band VNA extender lists ~$29,000.2728

Improvement: big non-solid-state machines (gyrotrons, free-electron lasers) make high THz power. Works for: plenty of power, but huge and expensive, which defeats a compact practical setup.
In the geometry

Moding

A cable or waveguide is meant to carry the signal as one tidy wave pattern (one "mode"). But once the channel is about as wide as the wavelength, it can also carry extra patterns, called higher-order modes. The signal splits among them, and because each travels at a slightly different speed they arrive at different times and smear together.8

It is like one lane of traffic suddenly splitting into several lanes at different speeds: the cars (your signal) show up jumbled. At 100 GHz the wavelength is just 3 mm, so ordinary-sized channels are already wide enough for this to begin.

some notes for me:

wave travels down the guide as one mode
--> the channel gets about as wide as the wavelength
--> higher-order modes cross their cutoff and start to propagate
--> energy splits across several modes
--> each mode travels at a different phase velocity
--> they arrive out of step and interfere, scrambling the signal8
From the environment

Sound & vibration

🗣️
phase modulated
talking / sound waves

Acoustic energy vibrates the cables and parts ("microphonics"); the vibration physically changes the path and phase-modulates the signal, smearing the reading.9 Under vibration, cables can add roughly 10-30 dB to the phase-noise floor (whisper vs shout is not cleanly quantified in the literature).29

Improvement: a vibration-isolation table and a quiet room (moderate cost). Works for: removes most microphonics; ultra-sensitive phase work may still need an acoustic enclosure.
The big picture

Alternatively, thinking about it as a wavelength

When your components and wires get comparable to the wavelength (3 mm), the voltage and current vary in phase along the wire itself, so you can no longer treat a wire as a single node.3

That is the jump from ordinary circuits to transmission-line and microwave design: a 3 mm trace at 100 GHz is a full wavelength long, so it behaves like a distributed structure, not a wire. It is a huge reason normal stuff stops working at mmWave, and it is a wavelength effect, totally separate from the skin effect.

Surface Roughness: a secondary result of the skin effect5

Once the current is confined to a small portion at the surface of the wire, the shape of that surface suddenly matters enormously.

No copper is perfectly smooth; rolled or plated copper has bumps a fraction of a micron tall. At 100 GHz the skin depth is only ~0.2 µm, the same scale as that roughness, so the current must follow every bump, which lengthens its path and raises resistance. That is why high-frequency boards use specially smoothed, low-profile copper.5

Improvement: silver-plate and polish the surface, or use litz wire (helps below ~1 MHz). Works for: below tens of GHz; above that no copper trick is enough, and the real escape is a hollow waveguide or optical fiber.

Key papers & results

Nature Photonics · 2021

Room-temperature THz laser

Khalatpour, Paulsen, Deimert, Wasilewski & Hu pushed a THz quantum-cascade laser to a maximum operating temperature of 250 K - a turning point, because earlier THz QCLs demanded cryogenic cooling. High-power portable terahertz laser systems, Nat. Photonics 15, 16–2013.

arXiv · 2022

THz source at −12 °C (261 K)

A follow-up improving the operating temperature to roughly 261 K by optimising the active region - moving THz sources toward thermoelectric-cooler-only, deployable operation14.

Metrology

Phase noise multiplies as 20·log(N)

A foundational result in frequency synthesis: multiplying a reference by N raises its phase noise by 20·log(N) dB. It's the reason a clean 10 GHz reference still gives a noisy 100 GHz tone6.

Interconnects

Copper waveguide ≈ 0.6 dB/km at 80 GHz

Dielectric-coated copper-tube waveguide studies report attenuation around 0.6 dB/km near 80 GHz, showing that guided structures, not open wire, make the mmWave band practical15.
