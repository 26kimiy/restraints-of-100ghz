Drude parameters for copper, silver, and gold

We opted for the DC-anchored route only because at 100 GHz the metal is a pure free-electron conductor, far below the optical and interband region, so every parameter follows from measured DC quantities (ρ, density, molar mass) with no fitting. Optical fitting will be implemented in version 2. Every input number (ρ, ρm, M) comes from one source, the CRC Handbook [1]; the equations are the standard Drude model [2]. Everything below is calculated; nothing is fitted.

ωp2 used (numerator of ε): Cu 2.70×1032, Ag 1.86×1032, Au 1.88×1032 (rad/s)2. The Drude model shows 100 GHz sits deep in the DC-conductivity regime: ωτ ≈ 0.016 to 0.024 (Cu to Ag), far below 1. The magnitude of σ(ω) = σdc/(1 − iωτ) is then within about 0.03% of σdc. Its one real AC effect is a small imaginary part (≈ ωτ, a couple percent of σdc), which shifts the surface resistance Rs from its σdc value by only about ωτ/2 ≈ 1%. The loss curves use this full σ(ω), so they stay exact; at 100 GHz it is within about 1% of the σdc value, and the gap grows to roughly 8% by 1 THz (up to about 13% for silver, which has the longest τ).

Calculating conductor loss - from σ to the surface resistance Rs

At high frequency the current crowds toward the conductor surface (known as the skin effect; click here for more depth), so the metal presents a frequency-dependent surface resistance Rs (the effective resistance the signal current sees). That single number is what every transmission-line conductor-loss formula needs [3]. With μ0 the permeability of free space (from the constants above) and the Drude conductivity σ(ω) from Table 3:

Rs = Re √iωμ0σ(ω)  [3]

At low frequency σ(ω) → σdc and this reduces to the familiar Rs = √(π f μ0 / σdc) ∝ √f. We evaluate it with the full σ(ω) = σdc/(1 − iωτ) (σdc and τ from Table 2), so it stays exact: at 100 GHz that is within about 1% of the σdc value, growing to roughly 8% by 1 THz (up to about 13% for silver).

At 100 GHz this is within about 1% of the DC-limit √(π f μ0 / σdc) (Cu 81.4, Ag 79.2, Au 98.2 mΩ); the two diverge above a few hundred GHz. Silver has the lowest Rs (best conductor) and gold the highest, and that ordering carries straight into the conductor loss.
